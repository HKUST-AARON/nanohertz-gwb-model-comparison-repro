What does this paper show?

The public NANOGrav 15-year Hellings-Downs free-spectrum evidence favors spectra with physical curvature or a characteristic scale over the canonical scale-free supermassive black hole binary spectrum. A first-order phase transition leads the tested physical templates, and environmental SMBHB hardening leads the astrophysical models.

Research questions answered

Does the NANOGrav 15-year spectrum follow the canonical SMBHB power law?

Xu, Zhang, and Guo (2026) compare the corrected NANOGrav 15-year Hellings-Downs free spectrum with the canonical gravitational-wave-driven SMBHB law. A free-slope power law gains Delta ln Z = 5.73, equivalent to a Bayes factor of 306.70 against the canonical model under the baseline priors. The measured spectrum therefore favors a flexible slope or a spectrum carrying a physical scale.

Which astrophysical model best explains the nanohertz spectral curvature?

Environmental hardening of supermassive black hole binaries is the leading astrophysical model in the comparison. The environmental SMBHB turnover model reaches Delta ln Z = 3.87 and a Bayes factor of 47.97 against the canonical scale-free law. Its bend frequency also maps the nanohertz spectrum to parsec-scale nuclear stellar densities, connecting the Bayesian ranking to a measurable property of galactic nuclei.

Which cosmological model has the strongest Bayesian evidence?

The first-order phase-transition template leads the tested cosmological models, with Delta ln Z = 4.94 and a Bayes factor of 139.46 against the canonical SMBHB power law. Under the baseline priors, it is the strongest physical source template in the compressed spectral comparison. The result identifies early-Universe symmetry breaking as the primary cosmological interpretation for follow-up tests of the nanohertz background.

How does the LISA forecast distinguish phase transitions from cosmic strings?

The orbit- and foreground-aware LISA continuation forecast propagates the PTA-selected posteriors into the millihertz band. The QCD-scale phase-transition posterior fades before reaching LISA frequencies, whereas the broad cosmic-string continuation remains LISA-bright. This contrast turns the PTA-to-LISA frequency gap into a direct test of spectral continuation and of any break required between the nanohertz and millihertz bands.

What can researchers use this work for?

  • Classify astrophysical and cosmological origins of a Hellings-Downs-correlated PTA signal.
  • Connect a low-frequency SMBHB turnover to parsec-scale nuclear stellar environments.
  • Compare first-order phase-transition and effective cosmic-string spectra in one evidence framework.
  • Test PTA-to-LISA spectral consistency and identify models requiring an inter-band break.
  • Benchmark prior sensitivity, low-bin ablations, leave-one-frequency-out stability, and alternative KDE products.
  • Reuse machine-readable evidence tables, posterior samples, scripts, and public inputs.

Analysis resources: the published evidence values come from public frequency-by-frequency KDE products. The repository also provides a direct timing-likelihood pipeline for full-likelihood extensions and independent follow-up analyses.

Frequently asked questions

What is the main conclusion?

Current public NANOGrav spectral evidence favors non-canonical structure over the strict scale-free SMBHB prediction. The result directs source interpretation toward environmental binary evolution or a spectrum with an early-Universe characteristic scale.

Which physical model is preferred?

The first-order phase-transition template ranks first among the tested physical templates. Environmental SMBHB hardening is the strongest astrophysical model. The stated model set, priors, and public KDE representation make this comparison reproducible.

What does the phase-transition result establish?

The phase-transition template is the strongest tested cosmological explanation of the compressed spectrum. This makes it the primary cosmological model for follow-up tests alongside environmental SMBHB hardening, the leading astrophysical model.
